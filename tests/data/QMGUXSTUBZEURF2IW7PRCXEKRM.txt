A federal judge on Monday dismissed a defamation suit filed by former Boston police commissioner Dennis White against the city and former acting mayor Kim Janey, ruling that none of the statements she made about him “were materially false — let alone deliberately so.” In a 74-page ruling, US District Judge Leo T. Sorokin rejected White’s claim that Janey defamed him when she fired him three years ago, made public remarks about her decision, and released an independent report into the police department’s handling of domestic violence allegations that had surfaced against him decades earlier. “White is understandably upset that Janey fired him” and unhappy that she believed the police department “suffered from a culture of silence,” Sorokin wrote. “He no doubt sincerely believes nothing about what transpired 20 years earlier should have impaired his ability to become Commissioner, despite Janey’s contrary conclusion.” However, he found that Janey did not defame White when she spoke truthfully about her opinions and her reasons for firing White. “He may feel his efforts to challenge the investigation and prevent his termination were appropriate; Janey, however, formed a different opinion, viewing his response as sending the wrong message and demonstrating poor judgment,” Sorokin wrote. “A municipal executive like Janey may publicly express her opinions on matters such as these — even when the object of the opinions disagrees with and is hurt by them.” The judge found there were “no material disputes for a jury to resolve” and dismissed White’s claims alleging defamation, violation of his right to privacy, and lack of due process. Those were the only claims remaining in the case after Sorokin’s dismissal two years ago of 11 others that had been part of White’s initial suit, filed in 2021. White’s attorney, Nick Carter, said in an emailed statement, “We respectfully disagree with the Court’s decision and will be considering Commissioner White’s appellate options in the coming days.” Noting that White was the second Black commissioner in the Boston Police Department’s history, Carter described him as someone who spent his career working to improve the community and was an advocate for police transparency and reform. “He had no findings of misconduct against him in his long career, and he continues to vigorously challenge the false allegations of his ex-wife, which she made during their divorce 25 years ago,” Carter said. “Those statements are contradicted by multiple witnesses who testified under oath. It is disappointing that after a 37 year career as a first responder in Boston, it can end like this, without a meaningful opportunity to clear his name.” Brian T. Kelly, a partner at Nixon Peabody who represents the city and Janey, said they were pleased by the dismissal and the case “has now been successfully resolved” for his clients. “As we have said from the outset, the facts of this case simply did not justify the federal claims they were making,” Kelly said. Then-Mayor Martin J. Walsh appointed White commissioner in February 2021, but he served for only two days before he was placed on leave after a Globe inquiry into past domestic violence allegations. White was accused in 1999 of striking and threatening to shoot his then-wife, also a Boston police officer, as well as hitting a 19-year-old woman in a separate incident in 1993. Walsh, who was preparing to leave office for Washington D.C., had hastily appointed White police commissioner without conducting any sort of vetting process. He ordered an independent investigation of the allegations once they resurfaced. Janey succeeded Walsh to become acting mayor and fired White in June 2021, citing White’s own admissions that he had pushed and hit members of his household years earlier. She said the allegations and evidence raised serious questions about his fitness to lead the Boston police. Janey ran for a full mayoral term, but was eliminated in the September 2021 primary. Two months later, Michelle Wu won the general election and was sworn in that same month. In Monday’s ruling, Sorokin also concluded that Janey’s release of the report detailing the independent investigation into the allegations against White did not violate the Privacy Act. “The investigation and White’s status as Commissioner were matters of public concern,” wrote Sorokin, who noted that “the relevant events” began with a media inquiry about the prior allegations against White. The media consistently raised questions about the sufficiency of the vetting process that led to White’s selection as commissioner, Sorokin wrote. During the same period, there was ongoing public attention to police accountability and transparency, he wrote. “In this context, the release of the Report was reasonable,” Sorokin wrote. “No factfinder could conclude otherwise.” The judge also rejected White’s claim that he was entitled to a public and evidentiary name-clearing hearing, which he asserted the city failed to provide. Sorokin found that White was given an opportunity to defend himself and respond to all allegations during the independent investigation, and his lawyer held a press conference following the report’s release.
