Spend enough time around Beacon Hill or Capitol Hill, and you’d think artificial intelligence is either about to save the economy or destroy civilization. Some Massachusetts voters seem considerably less worked up. A Suffolk University/Boston Globe poll out this week found that a plurality of Massachusetts voters are more intrigued than intimidated by the rapidly evolving technology. More than 36 percent said they’re optimistic that AI will help them do their work or function better, while just under 25 percent said they’re worried about AI taking their job. Then, there’s a whole segment who say they just aren’t, well, sure at all. Nearly 19 percent said they still don’t know how to use AI. Another 20 percent said they were undecided. The question itself is loaded, said Robert Owens, a biochemist from Rehoboth and one of the 500 voters surveyed in the poll. It’s almost “Like asking ‘how do you feel about money?’” he said. “Everyone uses it, but what they use it for really varies.” The results land at a moment when lawmakers are racing to regulate it, harness it, or are generally panicking about how it’s being regulated or harnessed.  Governor Maura Healey and state Economic Development Secretary Eric Paley have been selling Massachusetts as an AI hub. Healey unveiled a ChatGPT-powered AI assistant for state workers at an event that OpenAI chief executive Sam Altman beamed into remotely earlier this year. Meanwhile, House lawmakers in February passed legislation designed to regulate AI-generated content in campaign materials, though their interest hasn’t stopped candidates and campaigns from dabbling. In D.C., it’s proving to be a political minefield, as US Representative Lori Trahan is learning. The so-called Great American AI Act that Trahan helped craft with California Republican Jay Obernolte would impose safety requirements on developers of AI. But it’s facing heavy scrutiny from local lawmakers and advocacy groups from across the country. More than 200 state lawmakers signed a letter opposing part of the bill, including Massachusetts state Senator Great American AI Act and state Representatives Tara Hong, David LeBoeuf and Lindsay Sabadosa, arguing it would undermine efforts at the state level to regulate the rapidly growing industry. And more than 130 organizations — including AFSCME and Common Cause — followed with a letter of their own Thursday urging congressional leaders to kill the legislation that they warned could “freeze” state regulation of AI development and set off a string of lawsuits overturning existing state statutes. Trahan, meanwhile, is pushing back. In a fundraising email Wednesday, she pointed supporters to a CommonWealth Beacon op-ed she authored last month arguing that her draft legislation doesn’t overpower, but “elevates the strongest provisions from the states.” Torn on what’s the right move?
